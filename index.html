Analysis — Point 1

Which Technologies Does the IF Actually Fund?

Going beyond sector labels, the descriptions of the 53 selected & signed projects reveal a clear technology hierarchy. CCS dominates capital allocation, followed by Battery Materials and e-Fuels — each cluster reflecting where commercial risk is highest and EU market failure most acute.

    CCS dominates — and it is almost entirely a cement & lime story

    Of the 10 signed CCS projects, 6 are in the cement or lime sector (DREAM, ANTHEMIS, VAIA, CARBON HUB CPT01, AirvaultGoCO2, Leopard) and account for €805M of the €1,070M CCS total. Their descriptions share a single unavoidable constraint: limestone calcination releases CO₂ that cannot be eliminated by switching fuels or electrifying kilns. Carbon capture is the only route. The remaining 4 are CO₂ infrastructure: APOLLOCO2-LT (liquefaction export terminal using cold energy from LNG regasification, Greece); SLALOM 2.0 (gas fermentation converts industrial off-gases to ethanol, CO₂ tail gas stored offshore, Norway — classified as Pilot); HuCCSar (Poland's first onshore CO₂ storage pipeline, Pilot); and COnet2 Sea (medium-pressure LCO₂ vessel transporting captured CO₂ from Spanish cement plants to geological storage, Medium scale). The CO₂ infrastructure projects are spread across Large, Medium and Pilot tiers — showing the IF is building a full CCS value chain, not just capture units.

    e-Fuels: 15% of capital, but the most strategically deliberate cluster

    Seven signed projects deploy electrolysis to convert renewable electricity into fuels or chemicals. Four produce sustainable aviation fuel: ENDOR (Denmark, Large) uses gas-to-liquid synthesis producing 80,000 t/yr eSAF; TAKE KAIR (France, Pilot) applies RWGS + Fischer-Tropsch producing 39,000 t/yr e-kerosene — classified as pilot because the RWGS + FT route at this scale has not been commercially validated; ReSTart and DEZiR (both France, both Large, both Verso Energy) use identical 300 MW PEM electrolysers + biogenic CO₂ + Methanol-to-Jet to produce 78,000 t/yr eSAF each. The IF explicitly funded the same plant design twice, making this a deliberate replication bet. LUXIA (Spain, Large) stands apart: a 100 MW alkaline electrolyser feeding both a methanol and an ammonia plant for maritime and fertiliser markets. RECLAIM (Denmark, Medium) and PP2XH (Estonia, Medium) produce renewable methanol at smaller scale. No project in this cluster involves building hydrogen distribution infrastructure — hydrogen is always converted immediately into a product with an established offtake market.

    Battery materials: the IF is funding the supply chain, not the cells

    Five signed projects receive €446M, but none manufactures battery cells. All four Manufacturing-scale battery projects address the extremities of the value chain: POLVOLT (Poland) processes 80,000 t/yr black mass via combined pyro- and hydrometallurgy; REC2pCAM (France) integrates black-mass refining directly with pCAM synthesis in one plant; NEXTGEN CAM (Portugal) manufactures cobalt-free LNMO cathode material at first industrial scale; SB-VARKAUS (Finland) makes SuperBatteries with a niobium-oxide anode for grid frequency regulation. The fifth project, NEXT HYDROMET (Finland, Pilot), demonstrates a novel chromatographic separation for battery-grade metal recovery from 3,000 t/yr of black mass — classified as pilot because the chromatographic chemistry at this scale requires commercial validation before rollout. Cell gigafactory investment is absent from the signed portfolio. Whether this reflects a deliberate IF strategy, the absence of eligible applications, or other factors is not disclosed in the available documents.

    What the descriptions reveal as absent or constrained

    Green steel is absent from the signed portfolio — the only steel-adjacent project (SCW Steel CleanUp, slag chemical recycling) withdrew from the Medium scale section. Standalone hydrogen production infrastructure is represented only by H2PEARL (PEM electrolyser stacks, Manufacturing), which withdrew. Hydrogen as a final product does appear in two signed projects: ODIN (Norway/UK, Pilot) retrofits 15 Cessna 208B aircraft with ZA600 PEM fuel-cell powertrains across 15 Norwegian airports; and RjukanLH2 (Norway, Pilot) builds a liquid hydrogen supply chain — novel liquefaction, cryogenic storage, bunkering tower and four LH₂-powered shortsea vessels. Both are at Pilot scale, both operate within a defined national jurisdiction with committed operators, and both convert hydrogen into propulsion rather than requiring separate hydrogen retail infrastructure. Long-duration grid storage at meaningful scale is limited: LEYMEL (Small, floating PV + pumped-hydro hybrid) and the SB-VARKAUS SuperBattery for grid frequency services, but nothing at large commercial grid-storage scale.

    Analysis — Point 2

    Technology, Sector & Scale: How They Relate

    The five IF scale categories each contain a consistent and distinct technology mix. This is not coincidence — it reflects the capital requirements, deployment risk, and commercial readiness that the project descriptions themselves disclose. With corrected scales derived directly from the PDF section headers, the patterns become more precise than earlier analyses suggested.

    Large Scale (10 signed, avg €125.8M): exclusively CCS or e-Fuels

    Every large-scale signed project falls into one of two technology families. Six are CCS for cement and lime: each description details a capture unit (oxyfuel, cryogenic, or amine-based), a CO₂ conditioning step, and a pipeline route to geological storage. One is CO₂ export infrastructure: APOLLOCO2-LT (Greece) describes a liquefaction terminal using LNG regasification cold energy to liquefy CO₂ from industrial emitters across South-East Europe. Three are e-SAF: ENDOR uses gas-to-liquid synthesis; ReSTart and DEZiR use identical 300 MW PEM + Methanol-to-Jet chains. LUXIA sits as a large-scale Power-to-X producing methanol and ammonia rather than aviation fuel. Nothing in the Large scale category is a pilot, a recycling project, a biofuel plant, or a heat technology. Observed pattern: every large-scale signed project falls into CCS or e-fuels. This suggests that these technologies — given their full-chain capital intensity — may be the only ones that make economic sense at this scale, though other technologies could also have applied for Large classification.

    Manufacturing Scale (10 signed + 1 signed-only, avg €70.4M): industrial components that enable the transition

    Manufacturing-scale projects do not produce clean energy directly — their descriptions in every case propose a factory whose output enables other sectors to decarbonise. Four are battery or critical raw material facilities: POLVOLT (€150.7M, black-mass recycling), REC2pCAM (€125M, pCAM synthesis), SB-VARKAUS (€79.2M, SuperBattery cells), NEXTGEN CAM (€50.7M, LNMO cathode). Four are wind energy component factories: MEADOW (€109M, offshore wind turbines), Fos3F (€74.2M, concrete floating offshore foundations), VB1F (€48.2M, wooden turbine blades), Higher Ground (€39.1M, wooden towers >150m — signed-only, likely renamed from Trollhattan). Two are industrial enabling components: BIO-MEMBRANE (€14.3M, hollow-fibre membranes for biogas upgrading) and GRIP (€13.2M, Rotation Heat Pumps for industrial heat). ENGINEUS PULL, which builds an electric aircraft motor production line, is correctly classified as Small scale per the PDF — its €14.7M grant and semi-automated assembly line sit in a different tier to the large serial factories in Manufacturing.

    Pilot Scale (8 signed, avg €33.8M): the most analytically significant revision

    The corrected data reveals Pilot as a much richer category than the 2-project view from before. Eight signed projects carry this classification, and their descriptions share a specific logic: each proposes a first commercial deployment of a technology configuration that has not been validated at this scale, in this jurisdiction, or in this precise integration. ASTRA PP (Belgium, €40M) is the EU's first polypropylene dissolution recycling plant at industrial scale. TAKE KAIR (France, €40M) is the first RWGS + Fischer-Tropsch e-SAF plant at industrial scale. GESIIS (Italy, €31.1M) combines deep geothermal doublets with aquifer thermal storage and a heat pump — first of this integrated configuration. NEXT HYDROMET (Finland, €40M) demonstrates chromatographic separation for battery metal recovery. ODIN (Norway/UK, €21.4M) retrofits 15 aircraft with PEM fuel-cell powertrains across a live commercial aviation network. RjukanLH2 (Norway, €31.5M) builds a full LH₂ supply chain including novel liquefaction and cryogenic bunkering for four vessels. SLALOM 2.0 (Norway, €40M) integrates gas fermentation with CO₂ liquefaction and North Sea storage on one site. HuCCSar (Poland, €26.5M) demonstrates Poland's first onshore CO₂ storage pipeline. Notably, the Pilot average (€33.8M) exceeds the Medium average (€24.4M) — these are not small tests but substantial investments in first-of-kind commercial configurations.

    Medium Scale (17 signed, avg €24.4M): the fund's most diverse tier

    Medium-scale projects are the portfolio's broadest category at 17 signed projects, spanning: biofuels and biomass valorisation (W2BIOFUEL, VIPER, SCOOP, BIOVIND, GREENFIELD BIOGAZ — all using pyrolysis, anaerobic digestion or co-processing to convert waste biomass into fuels or biogas); CCS for lime and CO₂ transport (Leopard's membrane capture retrofit, COnet2 Sea's LCO₂ vessel); e-fuels at commercial rather than flagship scale (RECLAIM and PP2XH producing renewable methanol); circular economy (BMS bio-composites, Thermolysis tyre pyrolysis, ReNova ChemPET polyester glycolysis); industrial heat and electrification (Alpha One's Iron Fuel Technology, OTO's electrified plasterboard calcination); cement alternatives (MECACLAY's mechano-chemical clay activation); maritime zero-emission transport (eSeaRiverBarge's swappable-battery sea-river service); and agrivoltaic solar storage (NotNukeOne's 129.5 MWp agrivoltaic park with BESS, correctly Medium scale at €16.7M). The Medium tier is where the fund commercialises proven-enough technologies that are site-specific, biomass-dependent, or below the scale threshold that triggers Large classification.

    Small Scale (9 signed, avg €5.8M): first-of-kind configurations at minimal viable scale

    Small-scale projects receive modest individual grants (€1.8M–€14.7M) but each describes a configuration deployed at commercial scale for the first time. ENGINEUS PULL (France, €14.7M) — correctly Small per the PDF, not Manufacturing — establishes a semi-automated production line for the first EU-certified electric aircraft motor up to 125 kW; the modest scale reflects that this is a production line for a motor, not a serial factory for the motor's aircraft. LCSCM (France, €10.8M) builds a flash-calcination clay SCM plant. GREEN HEAT ASTURIAS (Spain, €8M) deploys PV + molten salt TES for industrial steam at a pharmaceutical facility. INSPIRE-PV (France/Spain, €5M) runs twin-site PV panel recycling recovering high-purity silicon and silver. Reprocover5.0 (Belgium, €4.1M) deploys a thermoset/thermoplastic composite recycling line. WasteLessDHS (Poland, €2.8M) connects data centre waste heat to district heating via a heat pump cascade. WATTRAXIS (France, €2.65M) demonstrates a novel floating PV configuration on a quarry site. LEYMEL (France, €1.8M) combines floating PV with pumped-hydro via a hydraulic short-circuit system for frequency regulation. Absorption Heat Pump (Germany, €1.9M) integrates an absorption HP into a waste-incineration turbine to supply 95°C district heating. The Small category projects are typically first-of-a-kind commercial deployments at modest scale. They appear to occupy a space between R&D and fully commercial investment, though the IF's own classification criteria are not publicly detailed.

    CCS & CO₂ forms the most linguistically cohesive cluster

    With 12 projects, the CCS cluster is not only the largest but the most tightly packed on the map — cement and lime projects share so much vocabulary (oxyfuel, cryogenic capture, CO₂ pipeline, geological storage) that they sit almost on top of each other. The notable outliers within the cluster are SLALOM 2.0 (gas fermentation + CO₂ liquefaction, Norway), MAGHYC and MCC2Hub (withdrawn maritime capture projects), which sit at the cluster’s edge. This reflects that CCS infrastructure projects share the storage and transport language but differ on the emission source, pulling them away from the cement core.

    e-Fuels splits into two sub-groups: electrolysis-to-jet and electrolysis-to-chemicals

    The e-Fuels & PtX cluster shows a visible internal split on the map. ENDOR, ReSTart, DEZiR and TAKE KAIR cluster tightly — all describe electrolysis + CO₂ conversion + Fischer-Tropsch or methanol-to-jet pathways targeting aviation fuel. LUXIA, RECLAIM and PP2XH sit separately, sharing the methanol and ammonia vocabulary but oriented toward maritime and chemical markets. RjukanLH2 (liquid hydrogen supply chain for maritime) appears at the cluster’s boundary, bridging e-fuels and hydrogen infrastructure language — consistent with its unique role as the only signed LH₂ project.

    Projects that did not execute grant agreements are not technologically isolated — they are embedded in the same clusters as signed projects

    A common assumption is that withdrawn projects represent a distinct or marginal technology category. The map disproves this. Most projects that did not execute grant agreements (hollow circles) sit inside or adjacent to the same technology clusters as signed projects. DEMONSTR8 sits within the Battery & CRM cluster alongside POLVOLT and REC2pCAM. MAGHYC and MCC2Hub sit within the CCS cluster alongside signed infrastructure projects. Trollhattan sits inside the Wind Components cluster alongside VB1F and Higher Ground. H2EAT, H2CWAY and ODIN share the H₂ Mobility cluster — but ODIN signed while the hydrogen-dependent ones did not. The technology was not the differentiating factor. Comparing the descriptions of non-executed and signed projects reveals a pattern: the non-executed projects more frequently mention reliance on external infrastructure — such as hydrogen distribution networks, multi-party port logistics, or specialist material offtake — that may not have been contractually locked in at the time of selection. However, no official documentation confirms that this was the actual cause of non-execution. Signed CCS projects, for instance, also depend heavily on pipelines and storage sites, but those were either already available or secured before signing. Without access to confidential grant agreement files, this remains an observed correlation, not a proven cause.

    Cluster boundaries reveal the portfolio’s structural gaps

    The map also shows what is absent. There is no green steel cluster — SCW Steel CleanUp withdrew and no peer project signed, leaving the iron & steel sector with only VIPER (biocarbon for metallurgy, sitting within Biofuels & Biomass). Long-duration grid storage appears only at the map’s periphery: LEYMEL (hybrid PV + pumped hydro) and SB-VARKAUS (SuperBattery for frequency regulation) are isolated, with no adjacent cluster suggesting a broader storage technology strategy. The Circular Economy cluster, while containing 6 projects, is notably scattered — reflecting that these projects recycle different materials (PP, PET, tyres, composites, iron fuel) and share little vocabulary beyond the broad circular economy frame. Whether these gaps reflect deliberate portfolio choices, a lack of mature projects, or other factors is not possible to determine from the project descriptions alone.
